## 0. Where the Title Came From
I borrowed the title from a lyric in `Plazma` by `Kenshi Yonezu`, from the thought, `"Would I have been happier if I weren't a developer?"`  
In `Mobile Suit Gundam GQuuuuuuX`, which is the background for the song, the protagonist [`Machu`](https://namu.wiki/w/%EC%95%84%EB%A7%88%ED%85%8C%20%EC%9C%A0%EC%A6%88%EB%A6%AC%ED%95%98) passes through a ticket gate because of a certain event and ends up living a completely different life.  
And after that choice, there is no going back to how things were before.

## 1. Resignation
At the end of 2024, I left my job. Around that time, burnout had already started creeping in.  
Data preprocessing work that I kept repeating without really understanding it, and backend development that I kept carrying alone.
The question, "Is this really something I should keep doing?" never left my head while I was still employed.  

There was also a more fundamental problem.  
The domain I was working in seemed to have a clear limit to its growth. The user pool was limited, and there were no signs that it would expand any further.  
The fact that there was no one actually using the features I built slowly started to wear me down.  

It's natural for there to be no users in the early days of a service,
but the moment you become convinced that "it probably won't grow from here either" is a completely different problem.
Unless you are the decision-maker, you cannot change the direction.    

In the end, if things kept going like this,
it felt like I would break before the service did.
So I chose to resign.

## 2. After Leaving
Once I had more free time, I also had more time to sit alone and organize my thoughts. So I decided to properly look at the problems I had. 
> * I wasn't sure whether I should keep going with programming.
> * I started doubting whether I was really interested in development at all.
> * I had no idea what I had been doing so far, or what I wanted to do next.
> * I thought that maybe the version of me before choosing to become a `backend developer` had been more comfortable.

Because of that, before trying to solve those problems, I first thought about why I had started programming in the first place.

### From the Beginning
* I first encountered programming in classes from my major.
* I became interested while learning R and basic Linux commands.
* After that, I looked for Python books and lectures, and at first I was thinking about data science.
* Later, I started doing PS through the recommendation of a close friend from the computer science department. [solved.ac](https://solved.ac/profile/hodako97)
* While doing PS, I discovered the world of the web, and during my military service I watched Open Tutorials lectures and started web development for the first time.
* After being discharged, I began studying CS and backend development, and kept developing with feedback from people around me using Java / Spring Boot.
* I joined a bootcamp, met many great people, and learned and grew a lot in a short period of time. You can find the records and retrospectives from that time on [the blog I used to run](https://isevou.tistory.com/).

I've liked making things since I was young.
> Lego, building PCs, games made with Adobe Flash (sadly, the floppy disk that had those games is gone now), making custom themes for the first iPod Touch I ever owned...

In the end, I wanted to make things, and I wanted to feel rewarded by seeing people use them. Sometimes I think that if I had discovered programming earlier, I probably would have made even more things.

If I had to sum it up,
> "I want to make something that people can actually use."

That was the biggest reason I became a developer.

### To Resolve Burnout
If you ask, "So how did you resolve your burnout?"
honestly, I don't know the exact method either.  

But there is one thing I know for sure.  
I went back into my own cave again.  

I just kept solving PS problems.  
I set aside my worries about programming for a while and simply kept solving problems.  
Almost like a machine.  

But thinking back on it, I needed some kind of driving force to begin again.

When you look at people who started programming from a young age, most of them have some kind of reason.  
Maybe they made a program to copy the password of a computer their parents locked so they could secretly play games.
Those small motivations eventually become the starting point.

So I decided to bring back the things I had wanted to do.

I went through the list of projects I had put off and decided to build them one by one.   
In that process, I made an open source library that converts DNA into natural language. 

You can find more details at the following link. [mercury](https://github.com/hodadako/mercury)

### Before Starting Again
In the end, I hadn't stopped because I hated programming. I had stopped because I had kept going without a reason.  
So when I started again, I wanted to do it not as "programming for the sake of programming," but from a place where I had a reason.

## 3. So Where Am I Now?

I still don't have certainty.

I still don't have an answer to whether this path is right,
or whether it's okay to keep going.

But there is clearly one difference from before.

If in the past I kept going `"without knowing why,"`

now I am in a state where `"I at least know why I started."`

So now, instead of choosing based on emotion,  
I am trying to choose based on criteria.

- Will someone actually use it?
- Is it a meaningful problem in the long term?
- Is it a direction I can sustain?

And based on those criteria,  
this is the direction I want to move toward from now on.

- Instead of simply making features, I want to make things that are actually used.
- Rather than work that ends quickly, I want to choose problems whose value grows over time.
- I want to make projects that are small enough for me to carry through to the end by myself.

I am still not in a completely sorted-out state.

But one thing is clear.

I no longer keep going without thinking.  
At the very least, I know the criteria by which I am making choices.

## 4. After Passing Through the Ticket Gate
In the story, `Machu` passes through a ticket gate because of a certain event and enters a completely different world.  
And after that moment, she can never go back.

Come to think of it, I was similar.  
There were definitely moments when I could have stopped, and it was a choice I didn't have to make.

And yet I passed through that ticket gate.  
Maybe that one choice is what determined my entire life as it is now.

If I had never started programming, would I be living a more comfortable life than I am now?  
Or,  
would I have remained in a state of being even less than I am now?  

I still haven't been able to answer that question.  
But there is one thing I know for sure.

I have already passed through that ticket gate.

I still don't know whether that choice was the right one.

But now,  
whether I will regret that choice or make it meaningful  
depends on what I do from here.

So for the time being,  
instead of worrying about whether "this path is the right one,"

I want to move in the direction of **making this path become the right choice**.
